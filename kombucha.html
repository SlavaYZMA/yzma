Teabag

Scheduled for September 2025

About

Teabag is an art-and-science project in which handmade bags made from bacterial cellulose serve as a model for sustainable design.

The material is grown through the fermentation of a kombucha culture (SCOBY), requires no toxic dyes or petrochemical components, and fully biodegrades within 4–6 weeks. These bags are not just fashionable accessories — they are organic artifacts combining functionality, aesthetics, and ecological thinking.

The project draws attention to the pollution caused by the fashion industry: synthetic materials generate up to 92 million tons of waste annually, and leather production requires up to 15,000 liters of water per kilogram.

Teabag offers an alternative: each bag requires less than 500 liters of water to produce and reduces the CO₂ footprint by 5 kg compared to leather counterparts.

Process

Fermentation

Bacterial cellulose is grown using Acetobacter xylinum over 7–14 days at 25–30°C. Ingredients: green tea, sugar, and organic SCOBY.

The result is a film made of 99% water and 1% nanocellulose, with a thickness ranging from 0.5 to 2 mm.

Processing

Drying, treatment with natural preservatives (wax, oils), and coloring with plant-based pigments:

Anthocyanins (beetroot)

Curcumin (turmeric)

Assembly

Manual shaping and layering, embedding of functional components: magnetic fasteners, lighting module, RFID protection.

Functional Features

Lighting: Built-in LED module (3.7V, 200mAh), two modes — 50 and 200 lumens.

RFID protection: Natural conductive fibers block 99.9% of signals (13.56 MHz).

Modularity: Expandable compartment (+20–30% volume), supports up to 20 kg.

Closures: Biomorphic folds with embedded magnets for concealed, secure fastening.

Environmental Impact

Biodegradation: 4–6 weeks

CO₂ savings: Up to 5 kg per bag

Water savings: Up to 95% compared to leather

Microplastic reduction: Accessories contribute up to 8% of global pollution [IUCN]

Support for manual labor and local production

Meets demand of 68% of consumers choosing sustainable goods [Nielsen, 2022]
